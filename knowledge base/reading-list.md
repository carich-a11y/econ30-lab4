# Reading List: Work From Home and the Motherhood Penalty

**Project question.** Working from home may help mothers of young children (less commute, more feasible jobs, easier response to home emergencies) or hurt them (more employer monitoring, stronger assumptions about productivity, perceptions of being less committed). Which effect dominates? "Better" is defined as **job retention** / labor-force attachment, with wages and promotion as supporting outcomes.

**Primary channel analyzed.** The workplace channel: the relationship between a remote-working mother and her employer, managers, and coworkers (monitoring, perceived productivity, commitment, promotion, wages). The household channel (partner and family expectations about childcare and chores) is acknowledged but out of scope.

**Method.** Quantitative analysis of existing datasets.

Legend: `[KB]` = already summarized in the knowledge base wiki; `[NEW]` = recommended addition.

## 1. Foundations: the motherhood / child penalty

These establish that the penalty exists and how it is measured. Keep as the project's base layer.

- `[KB]` Goldin, Claudia. *Career and Family: Women's Century-Long Journey toward Equity* (2021). The "greedy work" argument: jobs that overpay for long, inflexible hours make flexibility expensive and push couples to specialize. See `[[summary-career-family]]`.
- `[KB]` Kleven, Henrik; Landais, Camille; Søgaard, Jakob. "Children and Gender Inequality: Evidence from Denmark." *AEJ: Applied* (2019). Event-study method showing first birth is the turning point for women's earnings, hours, and employment. See `[[summary-kleven-children-gender-inequality-2019]]` and `[[summary-geography-of-child-penalties]]`.
- `[KB]` Correll, Shelley; Benard, Stephen; Paik, In. "Getting a Job: Is There a Motherhood Penalty?" *AJS* (2007). Experimental evidence that mothers are rated less competent and committed and are less likely to be hired. See `[[summary-correll-getting-job-motherhood-2007]]`.
- `[KB]` Kim, Hyunseob; Moser, Petra. "Women in Science: Lessons from the Baby Boom." Children reshape the timing of productivity, tenure, and survival in careers. See `[[summary-women-in-science-lessons-from-the-baby-boom]]`.
- `[KB]` Slaughter, Anne-Marie. "Why Women Still Can't Have It All." *The Atlantic* (2012). Qualitative account of how workplace structure, not personal choice, drives work-family conflict. See `[[summary-slaughter-why-women-still-cant-have-it-all-2012]]`.
- `[KB]` Han, Wen-Jui; Ruhm, Christopher; Waldfogel, Jane. "Parental Leave Policies and Parents' Employment and Leave-Taking" (2009). Leave can protect job continuity without erasing long-run penalties. See `[[summary-han-parental-leave-policies-2009]]`.

## 2. Remote work, careers, and promotion (core of the workplace channel)

The newest and most important gap. These speak directly to whether working from home protects or penalizes.

- `[NEW]` Bloom, Nicholas; Liang, James; Roberts, John; Ying, Zhichun Jenny. "Does Working from Home Work? Evidence from a Chinese Experiment." *Quarterly Journal of Economics* (2015). Randomized WFH raised performance and retention but **cut promotion rates roughly in half** — the central tension of this project in one paper.
- `[NEW]` Emanuel, Natalia; Harrington, Emma. "Working Remotely? Selection, Treatment, and the Market for Remote Work." Federal Reserve Bank of New York Staff Report (2023/2024). Remote call-center workers were more productive but promoted less; useful template for separating selection from treatment.
- `[NEW]` Emanuel, Natalia; Harrington, Emma; Pallais, Amanda. "The Power of Proximity to Coworkers." (2023). In-person work increases feedback and mentoring; remote work erodes it, with larger effects for women and junior workers — a concrete mechanism for a remote promotion/retention penalty.
- `[NEW]` Mas, Alexandre; Pallais, Amanda. "Valuing Alternative Work Arrangements." *American Economic Review* (2017). Workers' willingness to pay for flexibility and WFH; helps interpret who selects into remote work and why.

## 3. Flexibility stigma and ideal-worker norms (why remote work can penalize)

The mechanism that turns "flexibility" into a penalty: being judged as less committed for using it.

- `[NEW]` Cech, Erin; Blair-Loy, Mary. "Consequences of Flexibility Stigma Among Academic Scientists and Engineers." *Work and Occupations* (2014). Workers who use flexibility face career penalties and stigma.
- `[NEW]` Williams, Joan; Blair-Loy, Mary; Berdahl, Jennifer. "Cultural Schemas, Social Class, and the Flexibility Stigma." *Journal of Social Issues* (2013). Framework for how "ideal worker" norms penalize flexibility users, especially mothers.
- `[NEW]` Goldin, Claudia. "A Grand Gender Convergence: Its Last Chapter." *American Economic Review* (2014). Temporal flexibility and the pay penalty for non-linear hours; bridges the foundations to the remote-work question.

## 4. COVID, remote work, and mothers' labor supply / retention (outcome focus)

Context and evidence for the post-2020 expansion of remote work and its effect on mothers staying employed.

- `[NEW]` Collins, Caitlyn; Landivar, Liana Christin; Ruppanner, Leah; Scarborough, William. "COVID-19 and the Gender Gap in Work Hours." *Gender, Work & Organization* (2021). Mothers cut work hours far more than fathers during the pandemic — directly relevant to retention.
- `[NEW]` Goldin, Claudia. "Understanding the Economic Impact of COVID-19 on Women." NBER Working Paper (2022). Synthesis of pandemic effects on women's employment.
- `[NEW]` Barrero, Jose Maria; Bloom, Nicholas; Davis, Steven J. "Why Working from Home Will Stick." NBER / *Journal of Public Economics* (2021/2023). Source for the SWAA survey and the durability of remote work.
- `[NEW]` Aksoy, Cevat Giray; Barrero, Jose Maria; Bloom, Nicholas; Davis, Steven J.; Dolls, Mathias; Zarate, Pablo. "Working from Home Around the World." (2022). Cross-country patterns in remote work, including by gender and parenthood.

## 5. Data sources for the quantitative analysis

Where the retention and remote-work measures can come from.

- **ACS (American Community Survey).** Long-standing "worked at home" commute question plus employment and fertility variables.
- **CPS (Current Population Survey).** Employment, ASEC income, and telework questions (including the COVID-era supplement); supports month-to-month and year-over-year attachment measures.
- **ATUS (American Time Use Survey).** Where and when work happens, plus time spent on care.
- **SIPP (Survey of Income and Program Participation).** Longitudinal job transitions and fertility — well suited to a retention outcome.
- **Household Pulse Survey (Census).** High-frequency COVID-era data on remote work, caregiving, and employment.
- **SWAA (Survey of Working Arrangements and Attitudes).** Barrero, Bloom & Davis survey on remote work attitudes and arrangements.
