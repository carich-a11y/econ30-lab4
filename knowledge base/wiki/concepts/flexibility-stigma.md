Flexibility stigma is the career penalty workers face for using flexible arrangements such as working from home, reduced hours, or leave. The penalty comes not from lower output but from how the use of flexibility is read: as a signal of lower commitment to the "ideal worker" who is continuously available.

Williams, Blair-Loy, and Berdahl (2013) frame this through cultural schemas: workplaces reward an ideal-worker image of constant availability, so anyone visibly departing from it (often mothers) is judged as less serious. Cech and Blair-Loy (2014) document the consequences empirically among scientists and engineers, finding that flexibility-stigma beliefs are associated with worse career outcomes and dissatisfaction.

This concept is the bridge between [[face-time-culture]] and the question of [[remote-work-and-the-motherhood-penalty]]. If managers equate presence with commitment, then working from home can trigger stigma even when productivity is identical, turning a tool that should help mothers stay employed into a source of slower promotion and weaker job security. It overlaps with [[status-based-discrimination]]: both describe penalties applied on the basis of perceived rather than actual productivity.
