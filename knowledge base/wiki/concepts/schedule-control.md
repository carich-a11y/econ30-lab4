Schedule control is the ability to decide when, where, and sometimes how work gets done. In Slaughter's essay, it is the dividing line between jobs that can be combined with active caregiving and jobs that cannot. Goldin's work makes the same point from a labor-economics perspective by showing that flexibility has a price in occupations where employers reward continuous availability.

The article contrasts academic work, where Slaughter had more autonomy over timing, with high-level government work, where long hours, meetings, commuting, and constant in-person demands sharply limited that autonomy. Her broader claim is that schedule control can matter as much as total hours in determining whether a career remains compatible with family life.

This concept is analytically useful because it offers a concrete mechanism linking childbirth to earnings divergence. Workers with less control over their schedules may be more likely to turn down travel, promotions, client-facing roles, or office-intensive positions after children. Those choices can then compound into lower wages and slower advancement.

Slaughter treats remote work, smarter meeting defaults, and family-neutral flexibility rules as ways to increase schedule control without framing caregiving as a special exception. This makes schedule control a central variable for comparing occupations, firms, and countries, especially when thinking about why the motherhood penalty is steeper in some jobs than in others.
