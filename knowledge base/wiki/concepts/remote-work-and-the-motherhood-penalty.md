Remote work pulls on mothers of young children in two directions, and which direction dominates is the central question of this project.

On the **helping** side, working from home cuts commute time, expands the set of jobs that are feasible to hold, and makes it easier to respond when something urgent happens at home. Each of these can keep a mother attached to paid work instead of dropping out. Bloom et al. (2015) found that working from home raised performance and lowered quit rates in a randomized experiment, which is consistent with a retention benefit.

On the **hurting** side, the same arrangement can impose a penalty through the workplace relationship. Employers may monitor remote workers more closely (see [[remote-work-monitoring]]), assume they are less productive, or read a mother working from home as less committed precisely because she is a mother (see [[flexibility-stigma]]). In the same Bloom et al. experiment, home workers were promoted at roughly half the rate of office workers despite higher output, and Emanuel, Harrington, and Pallais (2023) show that being out of the office reduces the mentoring and feedback that careers depend on, with larger effects for women and junior workers.

This project defines "better" as **job retention**: whether a mother stays attached to paid work. Wages and promotion are treated as supporting signals of how durable that attachment is. The project analyzes the **workplace channel** (employer, managers, coworkers) and brackets the household channel (partner and family expectations) as a related but separate story.

The empirical version of the question is whether mothers of young children in remote-capable jobs show higher or lower retention than otherwise similar mothers in on-site jobs, and how that comparison shifted after remote work expanded post-2020.
