The motherhood penalty is not one single mechanism. Across the sources in this knowledge base, it emerges from several linked processes that operate at different moments in women's labor market lives.

First, mothers can face **status-based discrimination** in hiring and evaluation. Correll, Benard, and Paik show that equally qualified mothers are judged as less competent and less committed than nonmothers, which can reduce hiring chances and starting salaries before any actual productivity difference is observed.

Second, childbirth can trigger large **child penalties** in earnings through multiple margins. Kleven and coauthors show that first births produce persistent female earnings losses that can reflect changes in employment, hours, and wage rates. Later work also shows that the size of these penalties varies sharply across places and cultural groups, indicating that gender norms are part of the mechanism, not just background context.

Third, workplace organization shapes how costly care becomes. Goldin and Slaughter emphasize that when jobs disproportionately reward long, inflexible, or on-call work, couples have incentives to specialize. Mothers are more likely to move toward flexible but lower-paid roles, absorb interruptions, or slow promotion in order to remain available at home.

Fourth, penalties can emerge through **career timing and institutional evaluation**, not only through wages and hours. Kim and Moser show that mothers in science often remain highly productive but on a later timetable that conflicts with tenure systems rewarding early output. This means the motherhood penalty can also work through promotion barriers, delayed recognition, and attrition from elite professions.

Fifth, policy can mediate but not fully erase penalties. Han, Ruhm, and Waldfogel show that parental leave can support job continuity and increase leave-taking, but policy effects depend on eligibility, affordability, and actual take-up. Leave may change the short-run transition after birth without fully removing long-run earnings divergence.

Finally, these mechanisms sit on a historical foundation. Goldin's work on mid-century married women's employment shows that earlier generations faced more explicit exclusion from paid work. Contemporary motherhood penalties often look subtler, but they are built on a long history of institutions and norms that linked women's family roles to weaker labor market attachment.
