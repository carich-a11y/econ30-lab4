Motherhood and scientific careers refers to how children affect research productivity, promotion, and survival in science. Unlike many discussions of the motherhood penalty that focus on wages or labor-force participation, this concept emphasizes the timing of output and the structure of professional evaluation.

Kim and Moser show that mothers in science can remain highly productive, but often on a different schedule from the one assumed by academic career systems. Their productivity tends to recover and remain high later in life, while tenure systems reward earlier output. This creates a mismatch between when mothers produce and when institutions evaluate them.

The concept is important because it shows that motherhood penalties may arise even when women remain attached to demanding careers. In those settings, penalties can operate through promotion timing, selection out of the profession, and the loss of women whose later-life productivity is never fully recognized.
