Nonlinear career paths describe a model of professional success that allows pauses, plateaus, lateral moves, and later peaks rather than a continuous climb up the ladder. Slaughter argues that the standard model of success was built for a different era, when people had children earlier, spent longer in one job, and faced shorter life expectancies. Goldin similarly argues that if careers stretch longer across the life cycle, then temporary slowdowns during caregiving years need not be treated as permanent exit.

Her alternative is a life-cycle view of careers. Workers may want maximum flexibility during intense caregiving years and then pursue more demanding leadership roles later. She calls some of these pauses or slower-growth periods "investment intervals," emphasizing that they are not failures or exits but deliberate reallocations of time toward family or other long-run goals. Kim and Moser's study of mothers in science provides a historical example of why this matters: mothers can remain highly productive, but on a later schedule than institutions often reward.

This concept matters for the motherhood penalty because standard promotion systems often punish any departure from uninterrupted upward progression. If employers instead treated temporary slowdowns as normal, some of the long-run earnings losses associated with motherhood could be reduced.

The article presents this as both a cultural and institutional change: organizations can revise default timelines, tenure clocks, and expectations about age and leadership, while workers can plan around a longer career horizon. The concept matters because standard linear promotion systems often magnify motherhood penalties by converting temporary caregiving constraints into lasting career penalties.
