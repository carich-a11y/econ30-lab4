Gender norms and child penalties refers to the idea that the labor market effects of children are partly shaped by beliefs about how mothers and fathers should divide paid work and care. In this view, child penalties are not determined only by wages, taxes, or formal leave policy, but also by social expectations carried through families, communities, and institutions.

Kleven's work shows that child penalties vary enormously across geography and cultural groups, even within the same national labor market. Evidence from movers and immigrants suggests that a person's place of origin predicts the size of their child penalty, which is consistent with an important role for gender norms acquired earlier in life.

This concept matters because it helps explain why child penalties can persist even when women have high education and labor market access. If norms continue to assign women greater responsibility for caregiving, then mothers may face larger employment and earnings losses even in settings with modern workplaces and formal equality.
