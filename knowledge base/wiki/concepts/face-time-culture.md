Face-time culture is the workplace norm that treats long hours, physical presence, constant availability, and visible overwork as signals of commitment and value. In Slaughter's account, this norm is one of the central reasons professional women struggle to remain on top career tracks after having children, and Goldin's broader argument about greedy work helps explain why these norms carry such large payoffs.

The problem is not only the number of hours worked. It is also the expectation that work must be done on the employer's schedule, in the office, with little tolerance for school pickups, sick days, family emergencies, or limits on travel. That makes caregiving responsibilities more costly for workers who are expected to absorb them, especially mothers.

This concept helps explain a mechanism behind the motherhood penalty. If promotions, assignments, and reputational judgments are tied to face time, then workers with less schedule flexibility can experience slower wage growth, reduced advancement, or self-selection into less demanding roles even without explicit discrimination.

Slaughter treats technology, remote work, and redesigned default rules as partial remedies. The deeper point is cultural: if employers equate professionalism with uninterrupted availability, then even nominally gender-neutral workplaces will produce gendered outcomes. This makes face-time culture one modern pathway through which the motherhood penalty persists even after explicit barriers to women's employment have weakened.
