Parental leave and job continuity refers to the idea that leave policy can reduce the career cost of childbirth by allowing parents to spend time at home without severing the employment relationship. The mechanism is especially important in the months immediately after birth, when the alternative to leave may be quitting or taking a more disruptive employment break.

Han, Ruhm, and Waldfogel finds that expanded leave rights increase leave-taking, but the effects are uneven because eligibility and affordability are uneven. This means that formal rights do not automatically translate into equal protection across mothers. Better-educated and married mothers are often better positioned to use leave than single or less-educated mothers.

Kleven and coauthors suggests that policy can help explain short-run differences in child penalties across countries, but not all long-run variation. In that sense, leave is a partial buffer against the motherhood penalty rather than a complete solution.
