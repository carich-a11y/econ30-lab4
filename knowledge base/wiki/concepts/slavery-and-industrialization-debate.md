The slavery and industrialization debate concerns whether slavery was a central driver of Britain's Industrial Revolution or only a secondary source of wealth alongside more important factors. The classic pro-slavery-centrality position is usually associated with Eric Williams, while many later critics argued that slavery profits were too small or too ordinary to explain Britain's industrial takeoff.

Heblich, Redding, and Voth place themselves on the side arguing that slavery was more than a side story. Their contribution is to provide modern quantitative evidence that slavery-linked wealth had measurable local and aggregate effects inside Britain through capital accumulation and industrial expansion.

The main criticisms of this position are familiar. One is scale: slavery may have mattered, but not enough to finance industrialization at the national level. Another is causality: successful port and trading regions may have grown for many reasons besides slavery. A third is that strong local effects do not automatically establish that slavery was the decisive explanation for Britain's overall industrial breakthrough.

The debate therefore is not whether slavery mattered at all, but how much explanatory weight it should carry relative to technology, coal, domestic institutions, innovation, and wider imperial commerce.
