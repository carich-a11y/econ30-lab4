The historical normalization of married women's work refers to the long transition from an economy in which marriage and paid work were often treated as incompatible to one in which continuous female employment became increasingly normal. Goldin's historical work shows that this transition depended on changing labor demand, weakening marriage bars, and shifts in norms about whether married women should work.

This concept matters because the motherhood penalty has changed form over time. Earlier barriers often took the form of explicit exclusion from employment or strong social pressure against married mothers' work. In the contemporary period, the conflict is less often outright prohibition and more often lower pay, slower advancement, and greater pressure to trade career intensity for caregiving.

The historical perspective prevents treating the motherhood penalty as a timeless or purely individual phenomenon. It is rooted in institutional arrangements that have evolved, even if they no longer appear in the same legal form.
