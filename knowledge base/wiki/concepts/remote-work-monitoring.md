Remote work monitoring refers to the ways employers observe and evaluate workers who are not physically present: login and activity tracking, productivity software, message and response-time logs, and metrics that stand in for "being seen" at work. It is one of the workplace-channel mechanisms through which working from home could penalize mothers.

The concern is twofold. First, remote workers may be monitored more intensively than on-site workers because presence is no longer a visible proxy for effort, shifting evaluation toward measurable but incomplete signals. Second, when output is hard to observe directly, managers may fall back on assumptions, and a mother working from home may be assumed to be distracted by childcare or less committed even when her output matches a coworker's. This connects monitoring to [[flexibility-stigma]] and [[status-based-discrimination]], where judgments track perceived rather than actual productivity.

Monitoring also reframes the [[face-time-culture]] problem for the remote era: instead of physical presence, "always-on" digital responsiveness can become the new signal of commitment. The relevance to this project is that heavier or biased monitoring of remote mothers could weaken their standing, slow promotion, and ultimately threaten retention, even when remote work raises measured productivity (as in Bloom et al., 2015).
