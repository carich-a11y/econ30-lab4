Child penalties are the persistent earnings losses women experience after the birth of a child relative to men's outcomes or to their own pre-birth earnings paths. The term emphasizes that the key break often occurs at first birth, after which female and male labor market trajectories diverge sharply.

Kleven and coauthors show that these penalties appear across countries, though their magnitude varies. The mechanism is multi-channel: part of the loss can come from reduced employment, part from fewer hours, and part from lower wages or slower wage growth. This makes child penalties broader than any single explanation based only on discrimination or only on labor-force exit.

Kleven's later work on the geography of child penalties adds that these penalties also vary sharply within countries and are strongly associated with cultural context. Evidence from movers and immigrants suggests that gender norms play an important role in explaining why penalties remain much larger in some places and groups than in others.

Han, Ruhm, and Waldfogel helps clarify that policies such as parental leave may change the short-run transition after childbirth without fully eliminating the long-run penalty. Child penalties are therefore best understood as cumulative outcomes produced by family formation, labor market structure, policy design, and social norms.
