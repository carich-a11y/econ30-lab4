Status-based discrimination is the mechanism by which mothers are devalued because motherhood is seen as inconsistent with the cultural image of the committed worker. In Correll, Benard, and Paik's framework, mothers are not penalized because evaluators observe lower productivity. They are penalized because motherhood status activates stereotypes about competence and commitment.

This mechanism matters because it can operate before long-run wage or employment differences appear. Hiring decisions, salary recommendations, and promotion judgments can all be affected by assumptions that mothers are less serious workers or less available to employers. Fathers do not face the same penalty and may even receive a small premium from parenthood.

Status-based discrimination therefore helps explain why the motherhood penalty can begin at the point of labor market evaluation and then widen over time as those early judgments affect opportunities and pay trajectories.
