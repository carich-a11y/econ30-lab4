Greedy work is Claudia Goldin's term for jobs that reward long, inflexible, or irregular hours more than proportionally. In these occupations, being continuously available produces disproportionately high returns, which makes schedule flexibility expensive.

This mechanism matters for the motherhood penalty because caregiving is difficult to divide evenly when one job is structured to pay especially well for constant availability. Couples then face a strong incentive to specialize: one partner leans toward career intensity while the other becomes the reliable responder at home. In heterosexual couples, women still disproportionately end up in the second role.

Slaughter's account of elite professional work offers a qualitative version of the same mechanism. The problem is not simply ambition or total hours worked, but a work design that treats uninterrupted availability and visible overwork as proof of seriousness.
