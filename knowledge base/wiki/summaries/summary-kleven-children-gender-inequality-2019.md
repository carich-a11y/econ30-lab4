Kleven, Landais, Posch, Steinhauer, and Zweimuller (2019) compares child penalties across countries using a common event-study design around the birth of the first child. The key finding is that women experience large and persistent earnings losses after children in every country studied, while men's earnings are much less affected.

The paper's main mechanism is that children create a durable divergence in labor market outcomes through employment, hours, and wage-rate channels. The qualitative pattern is similar across countries, but the size of the penalty varies substantially. The authors discuss family policy and gender norms as leading explanations for that cross-country variation, while noting that parental leave and childcare appear more important for short-run dynamics than for fully explaining long-run differences.

This source is especially useful for your project because it shifts the question from whether children matter to how much they matter and through which margins. It also provides a comparative benchmark for thinking about whether policies can soften penalties or mainly change their timing.

## Key Claims

- First births generate large and lasting female earnings penalties across very different countries.
- Those earnings effects can operate through employment, hours, and wage rates rather than through one single channel.
- Cross-country variation suggests that institutions and norms shape the magnitude of child penalties.

## Limits

- This is a short comparative paper, so the mechanism discussion is suggestive rather than exhaustive.
- It focuses on average country patterns and does not fully unpack within-country class or occupational differences.
- It identifies child penalties broadly, not only discrimination or firm-specific processes.
