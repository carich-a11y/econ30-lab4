In `Career and Family`, Claudia Goldin places the motherhood penalty inside a longer history of women's struggle to combine career ambitions with family life. The book argues that the remaining gender earnings gap among highly educated workers is driven less by overt exclusion than by how modern careers reward long, inflexible, and on-call work.

The book's central mechanism is "greedy work." In occupations where earnings rise more than proportionally with long or unpredictable hours, couples have strong incentives to specialize. One partner becomes more available to the labor market, while the other becomes more available at home. Because women still disproportionately absorb care work, they are more likely to trade away earnings growth, promotion speed, and job continuity.

This source is especially valuable because it links historical change to contemporary inequality. Goldin shows that explicit discrimination and marriage bars mattered more in earlier periods, but that today the motherhood penalty often persists through workplace structure, caregiving asymmetry, and the high cost of flexibility.

## Key Claims

- The contemporary motherhood penalty is deeply tied to the organization of high-reward work.
- Couples often specialize not because women lack ambition, but because inflexible jobs make equal sharing costly.
- Lowering the price of flexibility is central to narrowing gendered earnings gaps.

## Limits

- This source is a broad synthetic argument rather than one narrowly identified empirical paper.
- Its strongest emphasis is on career structures for college-educated and professional workers.
- It is best used alongside more specific evidence on discrimination, leave policy, and child penalties.
