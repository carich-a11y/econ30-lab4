Heblich, Redding, and Voth argue that slavery was not merely a peripheral backdrop to British industrialization. Their central claim is that wealth generated through slavery and slaveholding was invested in Britain, expanded the domestic capital stock, shifted labor away from agriculture and toward manufacturing, and helped shape both the scale and geography of the Industrial Revolution.

Using compensation records, variation tied to middle-passage mortality, and a spatial model, the paper finds that places with more slavery-linked wealth had lower agricultural employment, higher manufacturing employment, more cotton mills, and higher property values. The authors estimate that slavery wealth increased British national income by about 3.5 percent, roughly the equivalent of a decade of growth, with much larger gains in the places most tied to slavery.

This paper is not the beginning of the argument that slavery was central to British industrialization. It enters a much older debate associated above all with Eric Williams's `Capitalism and Slavery` (1944), which argued that slavery and the slave trade were foundational to British capitalism. What is new here is the use of modern econometric methods and local spatial evidence to revive and strengthen that position.

## Debate And Pushback

The main pushback against this line of argument has long been that slavery profits were too small, relative to the British economy as a whole, to have financed industrialization in any decisive aggregate sense. Critics have also argued that returns from slavery were not uniquely high compared with other forms of commerce, and that Britain had stronger alternative drivers of industrialization such as coal, technology, domestic institutions, innovation, and broader imperial trade.

Another criticism is methodological: even if slave-linked places such as Liverpool became richer and more industrial, local effects do not necessarily prove that slavery was the main cause of Britain's overall industrial takeoff. Relatedly, critics worry that later compensation records may not perfectly capture the earlier flows of capital that mattered most in the first stages of industrialization.

In that sense, the paper is best understood as a major modern contribution to an old dispute. It does not invent the claim that slavery was central, but it pushes back against the older skeptical view that slavery was only a side story.

## Key Claims

- Slavery-linked wealth materially contributed to British structural transformation before abolition.
- The effects were especially strong in locations most connected to slavery wealth.
- Slavery helped shape the geography of industrialization, not just aggregate national income.

## Limits

- The paper focuses on Britain and does not by itself settle how general the mechanism is elsewhere.
- Some disagreement remains over whether strong local effects translate into a decisive national explanation.
- The broader historical debate still turns partly on interpretation, not only on measurement.
