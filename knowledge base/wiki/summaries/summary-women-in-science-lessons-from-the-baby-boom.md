Kim and Moser (2021) studies how children affected the careers of American scientists during the baby boom using rich biographical data linked to patents and publications. The paper shows that mothers in science followed a distinct life-cycle productivity path: unlike other scientists, they often peaked later, becoming more productive after age 35 and maintaining high output into midlife.

The paper's main mechanism is not simply reduced productivity, but a mismatch between the timing of mothers' productivity and the timing of academic evaluation and tenure. Mothers in science are held to standards that reward earlier output, even though many produce strongly later in life. This helps explain why mothers had much lower tenure rates and why many women were effectively lost from scientific careers during the baby boom.

This source broadens the motherhood-penalty literature beyond wages and hours by showing how children can alter the timing of creative and scientific output, promotions, and participation. It also highlights strong positive selection among mothers who remain in science, suggesting that observed surviving mothers understate the broader career costs of childbearing.

## Key Claims

- Mothers in science exhibit later-life productivity peaks rather than the earlier peaks typical of other scientists.
- Tenure systems appear to reward early productivity in ways that disadvantage mothers.
- Childcare access may help mitigate the career costs of children for women in science.

## Limits

- The paper is set in the baby boom era, so the institutional setting differs from today.
- It focuses on science careers and may not generalize cleanly to all occupations.
- Surviving mothers in science are highly selected, which complicates simple comparisons.
