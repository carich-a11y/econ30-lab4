Han, Ruhm, and Waldfogel (2009) studies how parental leave laws affect leave-taking and employment for mothers and fathers in the United States. Using CPS data from a period of expanding state and federal leave legislation, the paper finds that broader leave entitlements increase leave-taking, but do not clearly raise overall employment after childbirth.

The paper's main mechanism is job continuity. Leave policy can reduce the need to quit after a birth by allowing time away from work with job protection, but its effects depend heavily on who is actually covered by the law and who can afford to take unpaid leave. The gains are therefore concentrated among more educated and married parents, especially mothers, while less-educated or single mothers benefit less.

This source is useful because it complicates simple policy narratives. Leave rights matter, but they do not automatically eliminate the motherhood penalty. Policy design, eligibility, affordability, and gendered take-up all shape how much leave actually changes labor market outcomes.

## Key Claims

- Expanded leave entitlements increase leave-taking by both mothers and fathers.
- In the United States, those leave expansions do not clearly translate into higher overall post-birth employment.
- Policy effects are uneven because better-educated and married parents are more likely to be eligible for and able to use leave.

## Limits

- The paper focuses on leave-taking and employment, not long-run wages or promotion trajectories.
- The period studied predates more recent paid-leave reforms and post-COVID work changes.
- It highlights policy access gaps but does not by itself explain all long-run child penalties.
