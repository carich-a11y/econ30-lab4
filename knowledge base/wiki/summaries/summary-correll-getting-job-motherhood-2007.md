Correll, Benard, and Paik (2007) asks whether part of the motherhood penalty arises from employer discrimination rather than from measurable differences in productivity. Using both a laboratory experiment and an audit study of real employers, the paper compares equally qualified applicants who differ only in parental status.

The paper's central mechanism is status-based discrimination. The authors argue that cultural beliefs about motherhood conflict with the workplace image of the "ideal worker." Because mothers are presumed to be less committed and less competent, evaluators rate them more harshly, recommend lower starting salaries, and become less likely to hire or promote them. Fathers are not similarly penalized and may even benefit from parenthood.

This source is important because it isolates one mechanism that many wage regressions cannot fully separate: evaluative bias at the point of hiring and assessment. It therefore complements later work showing earnings penalties after childbirth by explaining how employer perceptions can generate or reinforce those gaps.

## Key Claims

- Mothers face lower perceived competence and commitment than otherwise identical nonmothers.
- Employer evaluations can translate motherhood status into lower hiring chances, lower pay recommendations, and stricter standards.
- Fatherhood is not penalized in the same way and may be interpreted positively.

## Limits

- The paper focuses on hiring and evaluative judgments, not the entire life-cycle earnings path after childbirth.
- Its strongest evidence is experimental and audit-based rather than longitudinal.
- It identifies one mechanism among several, not the whole motherhood penalty.
