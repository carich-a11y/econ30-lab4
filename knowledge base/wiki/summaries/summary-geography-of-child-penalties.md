Kleven (2025) develops a pseudo-event-study method that estimates child penalties using cross-sectional data rather than only panel data. The approach closely replicates standard event-study results while allowing much more granular analysis across time, geography, and demographic groups in the United States.

The paper's central empirical contribution is to show that child penalties vary dramatically across places and groups, and that this variation is strongly associated with gender norms. Child penalties fell substantially from the 1970s to the mid-1990s and then largely stopped falling. They are much larger in some places than others, and epidemiological evidence on movers and immigrants suggests that norms carried from place of origin have sizable effects on penalties after childbirth.

This source matters because it strengthens the case that child penalties are not only a function of market structure or policy design. Culture and gender ideology appear to be major explanatory variables, and the stalling of gender convergence since the 1990s may partly reflect the stalling decline in child penalties.

## Key Claims

- Cross-sectional data can be used to estimate child penalties with high precision through a pseudo-event-study design.
- Child penalties vary strongly across time, states, and cultural groups within the United States.
- Gender norms have large explanatory power for the size of child penalties.

## Limits

- The paper focuses on identifying the role of norms, not fully decomposing every economic mechanism behind the penalties.
- Some geographic patterns may also reflect urbanization, job structure, and flexibility differences.
- As with all pseudo-panel designs, identification depends on additional assumptions beyond those used in true panel event studies.
