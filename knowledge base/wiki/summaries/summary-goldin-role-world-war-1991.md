Goldin (1991) investigates whether World War II directly caused the lasting rise in married women's employment in the United States. Using retrospective survey evidence, she finds that wartime mobilization mattered, but less through permanent wartime jobs than through indirect changes that lowered barriers to married women's work after the war.

The paper's main mechanism is historical institutional change. War increased labor demand, raised wages, loosened social norms against married women's employment, and helped erode policies such as marriage bars. Even though many wartime entrants left work after the war, the decade helped normalize married women's labor-force participation and reduced the constraints that had previously kept them out.

This source matters for a motherhood-penalty project because it shows that the conflict between family roles and paid work has a history. Before the contemporary motherhood penalty took the form of earnings divergence after childbirth, married women often faced more explicit barriers to employment itself.

## Key Claims

- World War II was an important turning point in married women's employment, but mostly through indirect rather than purely direct effects.
- Demand shocks, wage changes, and changing norms all helped weaken earlier barriers to married women's work.
- The historical baseline for today's motherhood penalty includes explicit exclusion from paid work, not only later wage penalties.

## Limits

- The paper is about married women's employment rather than the modern post-birth earnings penalty specifically.
- It emphasizes historical transition and labor-force participation more than wages or firm-level promotion processes.
- Its evidence is centered on mid-20th-century United States history.
